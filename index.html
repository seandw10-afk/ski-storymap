The Big Idea

This story imagines a single epic winter route linking one headline ski destination to each continent. Some are world-famous lift-served resorts, while Antarctica is treated as a visionary expedition-style ski base to satisfy the seven-continent concept creatively.

The goal is not only to celebrate beautiful mountains, but also to explore the geography behind great skiing: latitude, altitude, prevailing storms, terrain shape, accessibility, surrounding settlement patterns, and seasonality.

This HTML file is structured like a polished web story and can be adapted into ArcGIS StoryMaps content blocks, or published as a standalone showcase page.

The Seven Resorts

Whistler Blackcomb — North America

Large-scale terrain, village energy, and maritime snowfall combine into one of the continent’s most iconic all-round mountain experiences.

Valle Nevado — South America

High-Andes positioning above Santiago creates dramatic scenery, thin air, and expansive terrain with big-mountain character.

Zermatt — Europe

Historic alpine village, Matterhorn views, and cross-border skiing give Zermatt unmatched visual identity and prestige.

Niseko United — Asia

Globally famous for deep, dry powder generated by cold Siberian air crossing the Sea of Japan.

Oukaïmeden — Africa

A rare African ski landscape in the High Atlas, where altitude enables winter snow above a markedly different surrounding climate zone.

Perisher — Australia

Australia’s biggest snow resort network, with broad terrain and a southern-hemisphere season that complements northern winters.

Aurora Basin Expedition Base — Antarctica

A creative concept resort: remote, wind-shaped, scientifically themed, and built around expedition-style skiing rather than conventional mass tourism.

Spatial Analysis of Location Factors

1. Latitude & Climate

Higher latitudes often favor colder, more reliable snow climates, yet altitude can compensate closer to the equator. This explains why Morocco can host skiing in Africa and why Andes and Atlas resorts depend heavily on elevation.

2. Altitude & Snow Preservation

Resorts with higher base and summit elevations generally preserve snow longer, support steeper alpine zones, and reduce rain-on-snow events. Zermatt and Valle Nevado benefit strongly from elevation.

3. Accessibility & Tourism

A great mountain alone is not enough. Airports, road corridors, villages, lodging density, and safety infrastructure all influence whether a ski area becomes globally competitive.

4. Relief & Terrain Variety

Strong vertical relief supports longer runs, while varied slope aspect can spread sunlight and preserve snow in different weather conditions.

5. Culture & Identity

Place branding matters. The Matterhorn silhouette, Niseko powder reputation, and Whistler village experience add narrative value beyond terrain statistics alone.

6. Environmental Risk

Climate volatility, wildfire smoke, thaw cycles, glacial retreat, and ecosystem pressure increasingly shape the future viability of ski tourism worldwide.

What Makes a “Perfect” Ski Resort?

A globally compelling ski destination usually balances four ingredients: reliable snow, memorable scenery, terrain diversity, and realistic access. The most successful resorts also build a strong sense of place through architecture, food, events, and identity.

That is why this story includes both pure geographic analysis and human-centered tourism factors. A map tells us where a resort sits, but the story explains why people remember it.
